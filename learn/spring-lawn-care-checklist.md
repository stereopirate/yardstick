Spring is the most consequential season for setting up the rest of your lawn year. The decisions you make in spring — when you fertilize, when you apply pre-emergent, whether you overseed — determine what your lawn looks like in July and beyond. The temptation is to do everything at once when the lawn wakes up. The research says: be patient, be strategic, and let soil temperature drive your timing.

## Spring activation vs. spring panic

Many homeowners see their lawn come out of winter looking thin, brown, or weedy and immediately buy fertilizer, crabgrass preventer, weed killer, and overseeding mix. Applying all of these at once is counterproductive:

- Pre-emergent herbicide prevents grass seed germination (don't overseed in spring if you apply pre-emergent)
- Heavy spring fertilizer pushes shoot growth at the expense of root depth
- Post-emergent weed killer applied to stressed, dormant grass can injure turf

Spring success comes from doing the right things in the right order, timed by soil temperature — not by anxiety.

## Cool-season spring checklist

These tasks apply to Tall Fescue, Kentucky Bluegrass, Perennial Ryegrass, and Fine Fescue lawns in zones 4–7:

**Step 1: First spring mowing (soil temps 40–50°F)**
- Wait until the turf is dry and actively growing before the first mow
- Mow at normal height or slightly lower (3 inches for fescue) to remove dead tips
- Do not scalp — this stresses roots recovering from dormancy

**Step 2: Pre-emergent herbicide (soil temps approaching 50°F)**
- Apply when soil temps at 2 inches are consistently approaching 50–55°F
- This is the most time-sensitive spring task — must precede crabgrass germination
- See the [crabgrass pre-emergent timing guide](/learn/crabgrass-pre-emergent-timing) for zone-specific windows

**Step 3: Light spring fertilizer (soil temps 55–65°F)**
- Apply 0.5 lb nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft after green-up is underway
- Do not apply heavy nitrogen in early spring — root development suffers
- Skip this application if your fall fertilization program was complete

**Step 4: Address bare spots (soil temps 55–65°F)**
- Repair areas damaged by winter, salt, or disease
- Note: If you applied pre-emergent in Step 2, bare spots cannot be reseeded for 3–4 months (check label)
- Use sod for spots in areas with pre-emergent present

**Step 5: Broadleaf weed control (when weeds are actively growing)**
- Apply post-emergent broadleaf herbicide when temperatures are 60–80°F and weeds are growing
- Fall is actually more effective for perennial weeds — spring treatment handles new annual broadleafs

## Warm-season spring checklist

For Bermudagrass, Zoysiagrass, St. Augustinegrass, and Centipedegrass in zones 7–9:

**Step 1: Wait for green-up (soil temps 55–65°F)**
- Do not mow, fertilize, or apply herbicide until the lawn is at least 50% green
- Premature spring activity on dormant warm-season grass stimulates weeds more than grass

**Step 2: Spring scalp mowing (when 50%+ green)**
- Lower mowing height 0.5–1 inch below normal and remove dormant material
- This exposes growing points to sunlight and accelerates green-up
- Return to normal height immediately after this single scalp mowing

**Step 3: Pre-emergent for summer weed control**
- Apply when soil temps reach 55°F (before crabgrass germination)
- For warm-season lawns in zone 8–9, this may mean February–March application

**Step 4: First fertilizer (when 50%+ green, soil >65°F)**
- Wait for the 50% green threshold — not a calendar date
- Apply at 0.75–1.0 lb N per 1,000 sq ft
- Bermuda: begin building to full summer nutrition program
- Centipede: one application only in spring at low rate (0.5 lb N)

## What NOT to do in spring

<div class="alert-warn">
<span style="font-size: 1rem; flex-shrink: 0; margin-top: 1px;">⚠️</span>
<div class="alert-warn-content">
<span class="alert-warn-title">Do not overseed cool-season grass in spring</span>
<span class="alert-warn-text">Spring overseeding of Tall Fescue or Kentucky Bluegrass sets seedlings up to face summer heat before they fully establish. New seedlings from spring seeding typically die in July heat, wasting time and seed. Penn State Extension is explicit: fall is the only reliable seeding window for cool-season grasses. The exception is emergency bare spot repair — use fast-germinating ryegrass for quick cover.</span>
</div>
</div>

**Don't aerate cool-season grass in spring:** Spring aeration for cool-season lawns opens holes that crabgrass seeds fall into — right when pre-emergent needs to be applied. It also disturbs the pre-emergent barrier. Save core aeration for fall.

**Don't apply heavy nitrogen:** Light spring nitrogen (0.5 lb N/1000 sq ft) is fine. Heavy application (1+ lb N) forces rapid shoot growth at the expense of root development heading into summer.
