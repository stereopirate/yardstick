Bermudagrass is the dominant warm-season lawn grass across the southeastern United States — tough, heat-tolerant, fast-recovering, and capable of producing golf-course-quality turf with proper management. It is also one of the most mismanaged grasses when homeowners apply cool-season care practices to a warm-season plant.

## Bermuda basics and zone range

**Taxonomy:** *Cynodon dactylon* (common bermuda) and *Cynodon dactylon × C. transvaalensis* (hybrid bermuda)

**Zone range:** Best in USDA zones 7–9. Can persist in zone 6 with good drainage and cold-tolerant cultivars. Cannot survive zone 4–5 winters.

**Key characteristics:**
- Spreads aggressively via stolons and rhizomes
- Goes completely dormant in winter (zones 7–8) or reduced dormancy (zone 9)
- Highest drought tolerance of any common lawn grass
- Requires full sun — does poorly in shade (less than 4 hours direct sun)
- Fast recovery from damage — one of the most resilient turf species

## Spring: green-up and activation

**When:** Late March – May (zone 8–9), Late April – May (zone 7)

Bermudagrass greens up when soil temperatures at 2 inches consistently reach 65°F. **Do not rush spring management** — early fertilizing before green-up stimulates weeds, not grass.

**Spring scalp:** Once the lawn is 50%+ green, mow at the lowest end of the normal height range (1 inch for common bermuda). This scalp mowing removes dormant material and exposes growing points to sunlight, accelerating uniform green-up. Immediately return to normal height for subsequent mowings.

**Pre-emergent:** Apply crabgrass pre-emergent when soil temperatures reach 50°F. In zone 8, this is often late February to mid-March. Do not wait for full green-up — crabgrass emerges before bermuda fully greens.

**First fertilizer:** Apply 0.75–1.0 lb nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft once the lawn is 50%+ green and soil temperature is consistently above 65°F. Use a 3-1-2 ratio fertilizer (N-P-K).

## Summer: peak management

Summer (June – August) is bermuda's primary growing season. This is when the lawn is most vigorous and most demanding.

**Mowing:** Common bermudagrass requires mowing every 5–7 days during peak summer growth to maintain quality. At 1.5–2 inch height, grass that grows 0.5–0.75 inches per week needs cutting before violating the one-third rule.

**Fertilizing:** Apply 1.0 lb nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft every 6–8 weeks through summer. Annual total for bermuda: 3–5 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft. Avoid single heavy applications — split feedings maintain more consistent growth and reduce burn risk.

**Irrigation:** Bermuda needs 1–1.5 inches of water per week during active growth. It will survive drought by going dormant, but drought-stressed bermuda is slower to recover and more vulnerable to weed invasion during the stress period. Water deeply (1 inch per application) rather than frequently.

**Aeration:** Aerate in late spring (May–June) during active growth. Bermuda recovers quickly from aeration and benefits from the improved drainage and root depth.

## Fall: hardening off

**When:** September – November

Fall management for bermuda is about preparing the lawn for dormancy and maximizing winter survival.

**Last fertilizer:** Apply the final nitrogen application 6–8 weeks before the average first frost. In zone 7 (average first frost mid-October), last N is late August. In zone 8 (November first frost), last N is mid-September.

**Fall potassium:** Apply potassium (0-0-60 at 0.5–1.0 lb K₂O per 1,000 sq ft) in early fall. Fall potassium improves cold hardiness and disease resistance through dormancy.

**Last mowing:** Mow at normal height for the final few fall mowings as growth slows. Do not scalp before dormancy — raised crowns and stolon material provide cold protection.

## Winter: dormancy management

Bermuda enters full dormancy in zones 7–8 when air temperatures consistently stay below 50–55°F. Dormant bermuda turns tan-brown. No management is needed or appropriate during dormancy.

**Foot traffic:** Minimize traffic on dormant bermuda, especially in low spots or areas prone to compaction. Stolons near the surface are vulnerable to mechanical damage when dormant.

**Winter overseed with ryegrass:** Optional cosmetic practice — overseed dormant bermuda with annual or perennial ryegrass in fall for winter color. Requires transition management in spring (aggressive scalping and/or herbicide to transition ryegrass out before bermuda green-up).
