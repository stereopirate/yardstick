Soil temperature is the only reliable trigger for pre-emergent timing in Bermuda lawns — not the calendar date, not the last frost, not what the bag says. Crabgrass and other grassy weeds germinate when soil temps at the 2-inch depth reach a sustained 55°F, and your window to stop them closes fast once germination begins. Miss it, and no pre-emergent will help.

For Bermuda grass grown across zones 7–9, that window opens earlier than most homeowners expect — often late February in zone 9 and mid-March in zone 7 — and it can slam shut within two to three weeks of warm rain.

## Why soil temperature drives the timing

University extension research consistently points to 55°F soil temperature at 2-inch depth as the germination threshold for crabgrass (*Digitaria spp.*), the primary target for spring pre-emergent applications. The University of Georgia Extension recommends applying before soil temps reach this mark and retreating if a second flush occurs in May or June.

Bermuda grass itself doesn't break dormancy until soil temps reach 60–65°F, which means there's a brief but predictable overlap window: the soil is warm enough for weed seeds to wake up, but your turf hasn't fully greened up yet. That's your target application zone.

Using air temperature or bloom indicators (like forsythia flowering) as proxies can work in cool-season regions, but they're less reliable in zones 7–9 where spring temperature swings are larger and soil warms faster after winter.

## Zone-by-zone application windows

These windows reflect average first soil-temp crossing dates based on NOAA climate normals and extension service guidance. Actual timing varies by year — always verify with a soil thermometer or a local extension office's monitoring data.

| USDA Zone | Target Soil Temp | Typical First Application | Watch For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zone 7a/7b | 50–53°F (trending up) | Mid-March to Late March | Cold snaps can reset — recheck after freezes |
| Zone 8a/8b | 50–53°F (trending up) | Late February to Mid-March | Often earlier after a mild winter |
| Zone 9a/9b | 50–53°F (trending up) | Mid-February to Early March | Second application likely needed by late April |

**How to read the table:** Apply when soil is trending toward 55°F, not after it gets there. A pre-emergent needs 1–2 weeks to establish a chemical barrier in the soil — if you wait until 55°F, you've likely already lost the window.

## Choosing the right pre-emergent product

Two active ingredients dominate pre-emergent use in Bermuda lawns: **prodiamine** and **pendimethalin**. Both work by inhibiting root cell division in germinating seeds — they don't kill existing weeds or affect established Bermuda.

**Prodiamine** (Barricade) is generally preferred for Bermuda lawns because it has a longer residual (3–5 months at standard rates) and lower risk of phytotoxicity. Pendimethalin is effective but breaks down faster and requires more precise water-in timing.

**Dithiopyr** (Dimension) offers a unique early post-emergent window — it can stop crabgrass through the 1–2 tiller stage, giving you a small buffer if you miss the optimal window.

### Application rates matter more than brand

Follow label rates for your lawn size. Bermuda lawns in zone 9 often warrant the higher end of labeled rates due to longer growing seasons and the need for extended residual. Underapplying is the most common reason pre-emergents fail.

North Carolina State University Extension recommends granular formulations for homeowners because calibration errors are less consequential than with liquid applications — a spreader setting that's slightly off is less risky than a spray concentration error.

## Second application: when and why

A single pre-emergent application rarely provides season-long control in zone 8 and 9. Extension research from Texas A&M and the University of Florida recommends a split application approach:

- **First application:** At the soil temp trigger window (see table above)
- **Second application:** 6–8 weeks later, before the first application's residual drops below effective concentration

In zone 9, crabgrass can germinate in multiple flushes from February through June. Without a second application, a late-season flush in May can establish before your turf canopy closes.

In zone 7, one well-timed application at the correct rate is often sufficient if Bermuda greens up and achieves good canopy density by mid-May.

## Water-in requirements

Pre-emergents require incorporation into the soil to work. The standard requirement is 0.5 inches of irrigation or rainfall within 24–48 hours of application. Without water-in:

- Granular products sit on the surface and photodegrade
- The chemical barrier doesn't form at the soil depth where seeds germinate
- UV exposure can reduce efficacy by 20–40% within 72 hours (per Purdue Extension research)

If you apply ahead of a forecasted rain, that's ideal. If no rain is coming within 48 hours, water in manually.

## What pre-emergents won't do

Pre-emergents only work on seeds that haven't yet germinated. They have no effect on:

- Established crabgrass (use a post-emergent like quinclorac or fenoxaprop)
- Broadleaf weeds like dandelion or clover (different chemistry required)
- Bermuda's own lateral spread — pre-emergents do not inhibit stolons or rhizomes in established turf

If you see crabgrass plants already emerging when you're reading this, skip the pre-emergent and move to a labeled post-emergent product.

## Bermuda-safe pre-emergents: what to avoid

Not all pre-emergents are safe on Bermuda. Simazine and some older atrazine formulations can injure thin or newly-established Bermuda. Always confirm the label lists bermudagrass (*Cynodon dactylon*) as a tolerant turfgrass before applying.

If you're planning to overseed with ryegrass for winter color, do not apply pre-emergent in fall — it will prevent the ryegrass from germinating. Transition back to ryegrass-free turf management by late August before pre-emergent applications resume the following spring.

## Application checklist

- Verify soil temperature at 2-inch depth is trending toward 50–53°F
- Check label — product lists bermudagrass as a tolerant species
- Calibrate spreader to correct rate for your lawn size (sq ft)
- Apply evenly; overlap passes by 50% to avoid skip lines
- Water in within 24–48 hours (0.5 inches minimum)
- Log application date and product in Yardstick
- Set reminder for second application 6–8 weeks out (zones 8–9)
