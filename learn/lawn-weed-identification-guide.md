Misidentifying a weed leads to the wrong product, wrong timing, and wasted money. A pre-emergent herbicide cannot control a weed that has already germinated. A broadleaf herbicide cannot kill a grassy weed. Getting identification right is the single most important step in weed control.

This guide covers the 12 most common lawn weeds across USDA zones 4–9, organized by growth type and season of activity.

## Why identification matters

Herbicides are classified by target type and mode of action. Before selecting any product, you need to answer three questions:

- **Is it a grassy weed or broadleaf weed?** Grassy weeds (crabgrass, goosegrass) require different chemistry than broadleaf weeds (dandelion, clover).
- **Is it annual or perennial?** Annual weeds complete their cycle in one season and are best controlled with pre-emergents before germination. Perennials return from roots and require targeted post-emergent treatment.
- **What grass type is your lawn?** Many broadleaf herbicides contain 2,4-D, which is safe for most cool-season grasses but can damage St. Augustinegrass and certain warm-season turf.

## Cool-season weeds

These weeds are most active in fall, winter, and early spring — thriving in the same temperature range as cool-season grasses like Tall Fescue and Kentucky Bluegrass.

**Annual Bluegrass (Poa annua)**
A light-green, low-growing grassy weed that germinates when soil temps drop below 70°F in fall. Produces white seedheads and dies in summer heat. Highly prolific seeder — one plant can produce hundreds of seeds. Pre-emergent herbicides applied in late summer (when soils cool to 70°F) are the primary control. NC State Extension recommends late August application in zones 6–7.

**Chickweed (Stellaria media)**
A low-growing broadleaf with small oval leaves and tiny white star-shaped flowers. Thrives in cool, moist conditions; common in shaded areas. Post-emergent broadleaf herbicides (products containing triclopyr + 2,4-D) control established plants. Dense turf is the most effective long-term preventive.

**Hairy Bittercress (Cardamine hirsuta)**
A rosette-forming winter annual that explodes seedpods in spring. Tiny white flowers appear in late winter. Difficult to control once mature — seedpods launch seeds several feet away. Hand-pull or apply pre-emergent in early fall before germination.

**Common Speedwell (Veronica persica)**
Spreads as a low mat with small blue flowers in early spring. Often indicates thin turf or overwatered areas. Post-emergent three-way broadleaf herbicides provide control. Improving turf density suppresses re-establishment.

## Warm-season weeds

Active in summer months, these weeds compete with warm-season grasses and invade thin cool-season turf during heat stress.

**Crabgrass (Digitaria sanguinalis)**
The most common summer annual grassy weed in the US. Germinates when soil temps reach 55°F sustained over 3–5 days; most germination occurs between 60–70°F. Light green with spreading stems that root at nodes. Pre-emergent herbicide applied before germination is the only effective control strategy. Post-emergent quinclorac or fenoxaprop work on young plants (2–3 leaf stage). See the [Crabgrass pre-emergent timing guide](/learn/crabgrass-pre-emergent-timing) for zone-specific application windows.

**Goosegrass (Eleusine indica)**
Often confused with crabgrass but germinates 2–3 weeks later (soil temps of 60–65°F). Distinguishable by its flat, silver-white stem center and zipper-like seedheads. Harder to control than crabgrass — it is more tolerant of preemergent herbicides. Dithiopyr post-emergent provides better control than quinclorac for goosegrass. Purdue Extension notes it thrives in compacted, poorly-drained areas.

**Foxtail (Setaria spp.)**
Three species: green, yellow, and giant foxtail. All produce the characteristic bristly, fox-tail seedheads. Germinate slightly later than crabgrass (soil temps 65–70°F). Same pre-emergent products control foxtail and crabgrass. Post-emergent control with quinclorac is effective on young plants.

**Spurge (Euphorbia spp.)**
A summer annual broadleaf with prostrate growth, forming mats in thin turf and cracks. Milky white sap when stem is broken — useful for identification. Post-emergent broadleaf herbicides control it. More common in compacted soils and thin, stressed lawns.

## Broadleaf perennial weeds

Unlike annual weeds, these return from roots each year and require persistent management.

**Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)**
Most recognizable lawn weed — bright yellow flower, jagged leaves, and a deep taproot that regrows if not fully removed. Post-emergent broadleaf herbicides containing 2,4-D + dicamba applied in fall (when dandelion is actively translocating to roots) are most effective. Penn State Extension recommends fall treatment as significantly more effective than spring treatment.

**White Clover (Trifolium repens)**
Three-leaflet leaves with white V-marks and round white flowers. Fixes nitrogen, which is why it thrives in low-fertility turf. Post-emergent triclopyr-based products (not 2,4-D alone) provide the best control. Consider whether your lawn is underfertilized — clover often colonizes nitrogen-deficient areas.

**Wild Violet (Viola sororia)**
Deep-purple flowers in early spring, heart-shaped leaves. Extremely difficult to control — a strong taproot and waxy leaf surface deflects most herbicides. Triclopyr-containing products at higher label rates, applied in fall when violets are actively growing, provide partial control. Multiple applications over several seasons may be required.

**Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea)**
Also called creeping Charlie — low-growing with scalloped round leaves, square stems, and purple flowers. Strong mint-like scent when crushed. Thrives in moist, shaded areas. Triclopyr is the most effective active ingredient. University of Georgia Extension recommends application in fall when the plant is moving carbohydrates to roots.

## Timing your treatment

**Pre-emergent control:** Apply before weed seeds germinate. For summer annuals (crabgrass, goosegrass), apply in spring when soils approach 50–55°F. For winter annuals (Poa annua, chickweed), apply in late summer when soils cool below 70°F.

**Post-emergent control:** Apply when weeds are young and actively growing. Young weeds have less developed root systems and cuticle wax, making herbicide uptake more effective. Perennial weeds are best treated in fall when the plant translocates nutrients downward — carrying herbicide into the root system.

**Conditions for application:** Apply when no rain is expected for 24–48 hours, air temperature is between 60–85°F, and wind speeds are below 10 mph. Avoid application during drought stress — weeds (and turf) need to be actively growing for uptake.
