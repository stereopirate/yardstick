A lawn renovation is a full restart — the decision to address underlying problems so severe that incremental overseeding cannot solve them. Knowing when renovation is the right approach versus when to overseed saves time, money, and a season of frustration.

## Renovation vs. overseeding: the decision

The key question is: how much desirable grass do you currently have?

**Overseed when:** Your lawn has more than 50% desirable grass species with reasonable density. You're filling in thin areas, repairing isolated patches, or improving variety. The existing root structure and soil biology can support new seedlings.

**Renovate when:** Less than 50% of your lawn is desirable turfgrass — the rest is weeds, bare soil, or undesirable grass species (like off-type bermuda invading a zoysia lawn). Also renovate when thatch exceeds 1 inch, compaction is severe, or you want to change grass varieties entirely.

Ohio State Extension research shows that overseeding into a lawn dominated by weeds (>50%) produces poor results — the existing weeds out-compete new seedlings even with optimal establishment practices. Full renovation with a clean slate is more effective.

## When full renovation is the right call

Signs that renovation rather than overseeding is needed:

- **Weed percentage >50%:** You're spending more controlling weeds than maintaining turf
- **Dense thatch (>1 inch):** New seed cannot make soil contact through thick thatch
- **Severe, widespread compaction:** Aeration helps but cannot fully remediate severe subsoil compaction
- **Grass type mismatch:** You want to convert from a mix of grass types to a single uniform species
- **Disease or insect damage is pervasive:** Widespread grub damage, dollar spot, or TAWC fungal issues require a clean start
- **Soil quality is severely depleted:** Heavy clay subsoil exposed by erosion, construction, or years of scalping

## Pre-renovation assessment

Before choosing a renovation method, document:

1. **Current grass percentage:** Walk the lawn and estimate the percentage that is desirable turfgrass vs. weeds/bare soil
2. **Thatch depth:** Cut a small plug and measure the thatch layer
3. **Soil pH:** Test pH and correct before seeding — new seed in extreme pH fails regardless of other care
4. **Compaction level:** Use a penetrometer or the screwdriver test
5. **Drainage patterns:** Identify any areas with standing water that need grading correction

## Total kill and reseed method

The most complete renovation approach: kill all existing vegetation, prepare the soil, and seed a clean lawn.

**Step 1: Total vegetation kill**
Apply a non-selective herbicide (glyphosate) when weeds are actively growing. Two applications 2 weeks apart ensure thorough kill of perennial weeds and their root systems. Wait 14 days after the final application before seeding — glyphosate breaks down in the soil and does not affect seed germination after this window.

**Step 2: Soil preparation**
Once vegetation is dead (1–2 weeks after herbicide):
1. Mow or scalp dead material low
2. Core aerate 2–3 times in different directions
3. Dethatch or power rake if thatch >0.5 inch
4. Apply lime or sulfur to correct pH if needed
5. Topdress with 0.25–0.5 inch of compost
6. Till lightly if soil is severely compacted or you need to incorporate amendments

**Step 3: Seed and establish**
Seed at the new-lawn seeding rate (typically 2x the overseeding rate):

| Grass type | New lawn seeding rate |
|---|---|
| Tall Fescue | 6–8 lbs per 1000 sq ft |
| Kentucky Bluegrass | 3–5 lbs per 1000 sq ft |
| Perennial Ryegrass | 6–9 lbs per 1000 sq ft |
| Bermudagrass (hulled) | 2–4 lbs per 1000 sq ft |

Apply starter fertilizer (high phosphorus, e.g., 18-24-12) at time of seeding. Drag a mat or lightly rake to improve seed-soil contact. Mulch lightly with straw or erosion mat.

## Slit-seeding renovation

Slit-seeding (also called slice-seeding) is an intermediate approach that doesn't require total kill. A slit-seeder machine cuts narrow grooves in the soil and drops seed directly into them — bypassing thatch and ensuring soil contact.

**Best for:**
- Lawns with 40–60% desirable grass where total kill would waste viable turf
- Thatch under 0.75 inch
- Areas where you want to introduce a new variety without full removal

**Process:**
1. Mow existing turf as low as possible
2. Core aerate to relieve compaction
3. Run slit-seeder in two directions at 45-degree angles, seeding at 75% of new-lawn rate
4. Apply starter fertilizer
5. Water as for any seeding program

Purdue Extension research shows slit-seeding produces consistently better stand establishment than broadcast overseeding in both compacted and normal soils.

## Post-renovation care

The first growing season after renovation is critical. Establish the lawn fully before introducing normal maintenance practices:

- **Watering:** Twice daily for first 2 weeks; once daily for weeks 3–4; normal schedule after first mowing
- **First mowing:** When new grass reaches 1.5x the target height — use a sharp blade and remove no more than one-third
- **Fertilizing:** Light application of quick-release nitrogen (0.5 lb N/1000 sq ft) 4–6 weeks after germination
- **Weed control:** Wait until new grass has been mowed 3–4 times before applying any herbicide
- **Traffic restriction:** Keep foot traffic off the newly seeded area for the entire first growing season if possible

## Common renovation mistakes

**Mistake 1: Seeding at the wrong time.** Cool-season renovation must happen in late summer/fall. Spring renovation of cool-season grass sets new seedlings up for summer heat stress before they fully establish. Fall gives them a full season to develop roots before the following summer.

**Mistake 2: Incomplete thatch removal.** Broadcasting seed over 0.75+ inch thatch produces poor germination. Seed needs soil contact. Dethatch thoroughly before seeding.

**Mistake 3: Not correcting the underlying problem.** A lawn killed by shade, excessive compaction, drainage problems, or wrong grass variety for the climate will fail again if the root cause isn't addressed.

**Mistake 4: Skipping starter fertilizer.** New seedlings establish root systems more effectively with phosphorus available. Starter fertilizer supports the early root development that determines long-term lawn quality.
