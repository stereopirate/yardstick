Post-emergent herbicides work on weeds that have already germinated and are actively growing. Unlike pre-emergents, which create a soil barrier before seeds sprout, post-emergents must contact or be absorbed by existing plant tissue. Their effectiveness depends on weed species, plant maturity, application timing, and environmental conditions.

## Pre-emergent vs. post-emergent: which do you need?

If you can see weeds growing in your lawn, you need post-emergent herbicide. Pre-emergent herbicide applied to established weeds is largely ineffective — the germination window has passed.

The decision tree is straightforward:
- **No weeds visible yet, spring approaching:** Pre-emergent before soil temps hit 55°F
- **Weeds already growing:** Post-emergent, matched to the weed species
- **Persistent perennial weeds (dandelion, ground ivy):** Post-emergent in fall for best systemic action

## Grassy weed post-emergents

Grassy weeds are the hardest to control post-emergence because they are closely related to desirable turfgrass. The available active ingredients are selective but carry a higher risk of turf injury if used outside label guidelines.

**Quinclorac (Drive XLG, Dismiss)**
Most widely used for crabgrass control. Effective on 2–4 leaf crabgrass. Works by inhibiting cell wall synthesis. Safe on Kentucky Bluegrass, Tall Fescue, Perennial Ryegrass, and Bermudagrass when used as directed. **Not effective on goosegrass.** Apply when daytime temps are above 65°F for best uptake.

**Fenoxaprop-p-ethyl (Acclaim Extra)**
Grass-selective post-emergent for crabgrass, foxtail, and annual bluegrass. Works by inhibiting the ACCase enzyme pathway. Slower-acting than quinclorac (7–14 days to effect) but provides more consistent control on larger plants. Safe on most cool-season grasses; avoid on warm-season turf unless label specifically permits.

**Dithiopyr (Dimension) — post-emergent use**
Dithiopyr is primarily pre-emergent but has post-emergent activity on very young crabgrass (1–2 leaf stage only). Beyond that stage, it acts only as pre-emergent. This is why early identification matters — dithiopyr applied at 3–4 leaf stage provides little post-emergent crabgrass control.

**Topramezone (Pylex) + MSO**
Newer active ingredient with broader grass-weed spectrum. Effective on crabgrass, goosegrass, and nimblewill. Often requires a methylated seed oil (MSO) surfactant. Can cause temporary whitening (bleaching) of turf; this is transient and turf recovers. NC State Extension recommends this as one of the best options for goosegrass control.

## Broadleaf weed post-emergents

Broadleaf herbicides are generally more forgiving because there is a wider metabolic difference between broadleaf weeds and grass species. They're also the most commonly used category for DIY lawn care.

**Three-way broadleaf herbicides (2,4-D + MCPP + dicamba)**
The standard product for most broadleaf weeds: dandelion, plantain, clover, speedwell, and common chickweed. Sold as Trimec, Weed B Gon, and many store brands. Safe for Kentucky Bluegrass, Tall Fescue, and Perennial Ryegrass. **Do not use on St. Augustinegrass, centipedegrass, or newly seeded turf.**

Penn State Extension recommends fall application (September–October in zones 5–7) for perennial broadleaf weeds — this is when plants are moving carbohydrates downward into roots, carrying the herbicide systemically for better control.

**Triclopyr (Turflon Ester, Ortho Weed B Gon for St. Augustine)**
More effective than 2,4-D alone for hard-to-kill weeds: ground ivy (creeping Charlie), wild violet, and clover. Also available in formulations safe for St. Augustinegrass and centipedegrass. University of Georgia Extension identifies triclopyr as the most effective option for ground ivy control. May temporarily discolor bermudagrass if applied during green-up — avoid until turf is fully established.

**Metsulfuron-methyl (Manor, Blade)**
Effective on hard-to-control weeds including nutsedge, wild garlic, and some clover species. Works more slowly (2–4 weeks to visible effect) but provides excellent systemic activity. Low use rate (often less than 1 oz per acre). Safe on most warm and cool-season turf when used at label rates.

**Season timing for best results:**
- **Annual grassy weeds (crabgrass):** Spring through early summer, while plants are young. Control efficiency drops sharply after tillering.
- **Annual broadleaf weeds:** Spring while plants are young and actively growing.
- **Perennial broadleaf weeds (dandelion, violet, ground ivy):** Fall is significantly more effective than spring. Fall application allows systemic movement into root systems.

## What to do if treatment fails

If weeds are still present 3–4 weeks after application, consider:

1. **Wrong product:** Confirm the active ingredient matches the weed species. Quinclorac won't control goosegrass; 2,4-D won't control grassy weeds.
2. **Timing:** Was the weed too mature at application? Crabgrass beyond the 4-leaf stage is significantly harder to control.
3. **Conditions:** Was there rain within 24 hours of application? Was the lawn under drought stress?
4. **Hard-to-kill species:** Wild violet and ground ivy often require multiple seasonal applications. A single treatment rarely provides full control.

When repeat applications are needed, observe the label's re-application interval (typically 28–30 days) and do not exceed the annual maximum rate listed on the label.
