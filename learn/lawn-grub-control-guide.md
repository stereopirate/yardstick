White grubs are among the most damaging lawn pests in North America, but most lawns with a few grubs survive just fine without treatment. The key is knowing when damage is likely, whether your lawn has crossed the action threshold, and which product to use — because the curative window is short and the preventive window is even shorter.

All white grubs share the same basic life cycle: adult beetles emerge in summer, mate, lay eggs in moist turf, eggs hatch into tiny larvae that feed on grass roots through fall, then larvae overwinter deep in soil and resume feeding briefly in spring before pupating.

**Why root feeding is so damaging:** Grubs feed on the fine root system just below the thatch layer, severing the connection between grass plants and soil moisture. Affected turf loses its ability to survive drought stress — hence why grub damage often looks worst in August and September during dry spells.

## How to Scout for Grubs

Scouting is the only reliable way to know whether treatment is warranted. Do not treat based on adult beetle sightings alone — adult populations don't always correlate with larval populations in your specific lawn.

**Best scouting time:** Late July through August, when newly hatched grubs are small (¼–½ inch) and feeding near the surface. This is also when treatments are most effective.

**How to do a grub count:**

1. Use a flat spade to cut three sides of a 1-square-foot section of turf (12" × 12")
2. Fold back the flap of turf and soil to a depth of about 3 inches
3. Count all white, C-shaped larvae in the exposed soil
4. Replace the flap and press down firmly — the turf will recover
5. Repeat in several locations across your lawn, especially stressed areas

<div class="alert-tip">
<strong>Sample widely:</strong> Take at least 3–5 samples across the lawn. Grub populations are patchy — one section may have 15 grubs while adjacent areas have zero. Average your counts across all samples.
</div>

## Grub Damage vs. Other Problems

Grub damage looks like drought stress initially — turf turns brown and wilts — but irrigation doesn't help. By late summer, patches may feel spongy or the turf rolls back like loose carpet because the roots have been completely severed.

**Secondary damage from wildlife:** Skunks, raccoons, and crows dig up grub-infested turf at night, creating ragged holes and overturned soil. Extensive digging is often the first visible sign of a serious grub infestation.

<div class="alert-warn">
<strong>Don't confuse grub damage with:</strong> Summer drought stress (bluegrass goes dormant but recovers), fungal disease (brown patch, dollar spot have distinctive patterns), or dog damage (high urea concentration, different pattern). Always scout with a spade before applying any pesticide.
</div>

**Diagnostic checklist:**
- Turf is brown but soil feels moist → suspect grubs or disease
- Turf peels back easily with hand pressure → strong evidence of grubs
- Wildlife digging at night → strong evidence of grubs
- Scattered dead patches, not following irrigation patterns → possible grubs

## Preventive Treatments (June–July)

Preventive grub products are applied **before** eggs hatch, targeting newly hatched first-instar larvae. They are far more effective than curative products but require correct timing — too early and the product degrades before eggs hatch; too late and grubs are already too large.

**Preventive application window: June 1 through July 15** (adjust 1–2 weeks earlier in warmer southern zones)

**Critical: water-in requirement.** All preventive grub products must be watered in with at least ½ inch of irrigation or rainfall within 24–48 hours of application. Without watering, the active ingredient stays on the thatch layer and never reaches the soil where eggs are laid.

<div class="alert-tip">
<strong>Preventive strategy:</strong> If your lawn had grub damage last year, or you see high adult beetle activity (Japanese beetles on roses and ornamentals, masked chafers flying at dusk), applying a preventive product in June–early July is reasonable — even without scouting — because the cost of a preventive application is lower than reseeding damaged turf.
</div>

**Pollinator considerations:** Imidacloprid and other neonicotinoids can harm pollinators if applied to flowering weeds. Mow the lawn to remove flowering weeds (clover, dandelion) before application, or choose chlorantraniliprole, which has the lowest bee toxicity of any grub product.

## Curative Treatments (August–September)

Curative products work on actively feeding grubs, but they are significantly less effective than preventive products — expect 50–75% control versus 90%+ for preventives. They are used when grub damage is occurring and the preventive window has passed.

**Curative application window: August through mid-September** — while grubs are still small and feeding near the surface. Effectiveness drops sharply once grubs exceed ½ inch and begin moving deeper.

**After curative treatment:** If significant turf was killed, overseed bare areas in early fall (cool-season grasses) or repair with sod. Grubs that survive the treatment may be present in reduced numbers — scout again the following August.

## Natural and Biological Controls

Biological options work, but have important limitations — they need live, actively feeding grubs near the surface and moist soil conditions to be effective.

**Beneficial nematodes (Steinernema glaseri, Heterorhabditis bacteriophora):**
- Microscopic roundworms that parasitize grub larvae
- Most effective on young (first-instar) grubs in moist soil
- Must be applied in the evening, watered in immediately, and kept moist for 1–2 weeks
- Efficacy: 50–75% in ideal conditions; less reliable in dry or hot conditions
- Refrigerate nematodes and use within their shelf life (usually 2–4 weeks)

**Milky spore (Paenibacillus popilliae):**
- Bacterial disease specific to Japanese beetle grubs
- Must be applied over 2–3 seasons for establishment; long-term residual once established
- Ineffective against other grub species (chafers, May beetles)
- Takes 1–3 years to build up effective soil populations

<div class="alert-tip">
<strong>Combining approaches:</strong> For organic lawns, apply beneficial nematodes in August for immediate knockdown of young grubs, then apply milky spore in fall for long-term Japanese beetle control. Maintain lawn health so turf can tolerate moderate grub feeding without treatment.
</div>

## Preventing Future Infestations

Long-term grub management is more about lawn health than repeated pesticide applications.

**Cultural practices that reduce grub pressure:**

- **Deep, infrequent watering:** Female beetles prefer moist turf for egg-laying. Allowing the top inch of soil to dry out during June–July egg-laying season can reduce egg survival — though this conflicts with turf health during heat stress.
- **Raise mowing height:** Taller turf (3–4 inches) is more drought-tolerant and recovers from root feeding better than short, stressed turf.
- **Improve soil drainage:** Grubs thrive in compacted, poorly draining soils. Annual aeration and topdressing with compost improves drainage and root depth, making turf more tolerant of feeding.
- **Overseed thin areas:** Dense, actively growing turf tolerates grub feeding better than sparse, stressed turf. Thin lawns invite weed pressure and have less root mass to lose.
- **Reduce thatch:** Thick thatch (over ½ inch) provides an ideal environment for grub development. Dethatching and aeration reduce favorable habitat.

<div class="alert-warn">
<strong>Don't treat every year automatically:</strong> Rotating or annual insecticide applications select for pesticide resistance and harm beneficial soil biology. Scout every August and only treat when grub counts exceed the action threshold for your grass type. Many lawns recover from grub feeding on their own with adequate irrigation and fertilization.
</div>

**After a heavy grub year:**
1. Repair dead patches with appropriate seed or sod
2. Fertilize to stimulate root regrowth (fall for cool-season, late spring for warm-season)
3. Scout again the following August — a heavy year does not guarantee repeat damage
4. Consider a preventive product only if adult beetle pressure is observed again in June–July

With correct timing, scouting, and product selection, grub damage is manageable — and many lawns with moderate grub populations survive without intervention when kept healthy and well-irrigated.
