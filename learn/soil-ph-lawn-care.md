Soil pH is the single most important soil characteristic for lawn health — more important than organic matter, nitrogen levels, or drainage in many cases. A soil pH outside the optimal range blocks nutrient uptake regardless of how much fertilizer you apply. You can spend $500 on fertilizer and get poor results on a lawn with pH problems that a $5 bag of lime could fix.

## Why pH matters for lawn health

Soil pH is a measure of acidity or alkalinity on a scale of 0–14, with 7.0 neutral. Below 7.0 is acidic; above 7.0 is alkaline. Turfgrass nutrients — nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrients — are most available to plant roots in the pH range of 6.0–7.0.

Outside this range:
- **Too acidic (below 6.0):** Phosphorus becomes unavailable; aluminum and manganese reach toxic concentrations; beneficial soil microbes are inhibited; grass becomes pale, thin, and slow-recovering
- **Too alkaline (above 7.5):** Iron and manganese become unavailable, causing yellowing (chlorosis); most nutrients are locked in insoluble forms

Penn State Extension research shows that lawns with pH corrected into the 6.0–7.0 range frequently show improvement equal to or greater than what a fertilizer application provides — simply because nutrients already in the soil become available.

**Centipedegrass is the critical exception:** Centipede thrives at lower pH than other grass types. Raising pH above 6.5 with lime is a common cause of centipede decline in many southeastern lawns. Clemson Extension explicitly warns against liming centipede without a soil test confirming deficiency.

## How to test soil pH

**Home pH test kits:** Available at garden centers for $10–20. Provide a rough reading (±0.5 pH). Useful for confirming that pH is in a reasonable range or obviously problematic.

**University extension soil testing:** The most accurate and informative option. Most state extension services offer soil testing for $10–20 and provide:
- Precise pH measurement
- Nutrient levels (phosphorus, potassium, organic matter, calcium)
- Specific lime or sulfur recommendations based on your soil buffer capacity
- Fertilizer rate recommendations

**How to collect a soil sample:**
1. Use a soil probe or trowel to collect soil from 10–15 spots across the lawn
2. Collect at 2–4 inch depth (the root zone)
3. Mix all samples together in a clean bucket
4. Fill the submission bag (from your extension office) with 1 cup of the mixed sample
5. Label with grass type and current management history

Penn State Extension recommends testing every 2–3 years, or any time the lawn is struggling without an obvious cause.

## Reading your soil test results

A university extension soil test returns pH along with a lime recommendation. The recommendation is specific to your soil:

**High-CEC soils (clay):** Require more lime to achieve the same pH change than sandy or low-CEC soils. The extension lab calculates this using a "buffer pH" reading.

**Low-CEC soils (sandy):** Require less lime per unit of pH change but are more prone to pH swings — test more frequently.

A typical low-to-moderate application recommendation might be "apply 50 lbs of ground limestone per 1,000 sq ft." This is a specific recommendation calibrated to your soil.

## Raising pH with lime

When soil pH is below 6.0 (acidic), lime application raises pH toward the optimal range.

**Lime products:**
- **Ground limestone (calcite):** Most common; slow-release over 3–6 months
- **Dolomitic limestone:** Contains calcium AND magnesium — preferred if soil test shows magnesium deficiency
- **Pelletized limestone:** Same chemistry as ground limestone in pellet form; easier to spread evenly; slightly faster-acting

**Application rate:**
Follow your soil test recommendation. As a general guideline, 50 lbs of ground limestone per 1,000 sq ft raises pH by approximately 0.5–1.0 units in most soils. However, this varies significantly by soil type — always follow test-specific recommendations.

**Application timing:**
Fall is ideal for lime application on cool-season lawns — the winter freeze-thaw cycle helps work lime into the soil. For warm-season lawns, fall or winter (dormancy period) application works well.

Can apply in spring if fall was missed; pH correction takes several months regardless.

**Lime application method:**
Use a broadcast spreader (drop or rotary) for even coverage. Apply to a dry lawn; water in after application. Do not over-apply — lime applied in excess of your soil test recommendation can raise pH too high, causing secondary nutrient problems.

## Lowering pH with sulfur

When soil pH is above 7.0 (alkaline), elemental sulfur lowers pH. This is more common in arid western regions and can occur in heavily limed soils.

**Elemental sulfur:** Soil bacteria oxidize it to sulfuric acid, lowering pH. Slower-acting than lime (4–6+ months for significant effect).

**Application rate:**
Soil type matters significantly. Applying 5–10 lbs of elemental sulfur per 1,000 sq ft typically lowers pH by 0.5–1.0 units in sandy soil; clay soil requires 2–4x more for the same effect.

**Limits:** Sulfur is most effective between pH 5.5–7.5. Very alkaline soils (above 8.0) are very difficult to acidify significantly — often due to free calcium carbonate in the soil.
