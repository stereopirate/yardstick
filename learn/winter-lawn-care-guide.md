Winter is not a management-free period. While your lawn is dormant or growing slowly, the decisions you make — and the damage that occurs — set the stage for spring recovery. The best approach to winter lawn care is largely one of protection: reduce traffic, avoid salt damage, and resist the urge to force anything.

## Cool-season lawn in winter

Cool-season grasses (Tall Fescue, Kentucky Bluegrass, Perennial Ryegrass) slow growth dramatically as soil temperatures drop below 50°F and stop active growth below 45°F. In zones 4–6, these grasses experience full dormancy — they remain green but are not actively growing. In zones 6–7, cool-season turf may continue slow growth during mild winter stretches.

**What to do in winter for cool-season lawns:**
- **Nothing.** Seriously. Winter is the maintenance-free period. No fertilizer, no herbicide, no aeration.
- Keep foot traffic minimal on frozen or frost-covered turf
- Clear snow if possible on high-traffic areas to prevent extended compression
- Note areas of dead or damaged grass for spring repair

**Dormancy vs. dead:** Cool-season grass that looks brown and flat in winter is almost certainly dormant — not dead. Pull a small plug and check for green crowns (growing points at soil level). If crowns are still green, the plant is alive and dormant. Brown crowns indicate potential winter kill.

## Warm-season dormancy

Bermudagrass, Zoysiagrass, St. Augustinegrass, and Centipedegrass enter full dormancy when temperatures stay below 55°F for several consecutive days. In zones 7–8, dormancy runs approximately November through February. In zone 9, dormancy is shorter (4–6 weeks) or sometimes absent entirely.

Dormant warm-season turf turns tan or straw-colored. This is normal and expected. The grass is alive — it will green up when temperatures and soil warmth return to active-growth thresholds.

**Overseeding dormant bermuda for winter color:** Some homeowners overseed dormant bermuda with annual or perennial ryegrass in fall for a green winter lawn. This is cosmetic, not agronomic — and requires transitioning out the ryegrass in spring before bermuda green-up. If you've done this, do not fertilize the ryegrass in late winter with nitrogen — it will compete with bermuda green-up in spring.

## Winter traffic damage

Walking on frozen or frost-covered turf causes crown damage. When grass blades are frozen, they become brittle — foot pressure ruptures cells and leaves permanent brown footprint-shaped damage patterns. This is one of the most common sources of "mysterious dead spots" that appear in spring.

**Rule:** Avoid walking on frosted or frozen turf until the frost has melted (typically by mid-morning to noon). Morning frost that melts by 10–11 a.m. leaves no lasting damage if traffic waits until after thaw.

On packed snow: foot traffic on packed snow over turf is generally low-impact if the snow is deep enough to prevent turf contact. Walking on shallow (1–2 inch) snow on frozen grass can still cause damage.

**High-traffic winter paths:** If specific areas receive unavoidable winter traffic (mailbox path, gate access), protect them with temporary stepping stones, pavers, or thick mulch over the path. Penn State Extension notes that turf recovery from winter traffic damage is slow — repair doesn't happen until spring growth is established.

## Ice and de-icing salt

Road salt (sodium chloride) and calcium chloride are the most common ice treatments. Both can cause significant turf injury:

**Sodium chloride (rock salt):** The most damaging to turf. Salt draws water from plant cells, causing salt burn. Excess soil sodium also degrades soil structure over time. Injury appears in spring as brown strips or patches along salted walkways and driveways.

**Calcium chloride:** Less damaging than sodium chloride but can still injure turf at high rates. Acts faster and at lower temperatures than sodium chloride.

**Safer alternatives for turf protection:**
- Sand or fine gravel: Provides traction without chemical burn
- Kitty litter: Traction without salt
- Potassium chloride (KCl, sold as Ice Melt with potassium): Lower turf impact; note that high rates still cause problems

VCE Extension recommends buffering salt-adjacent turf areas with extra irrigation in early spring to leach salts from the root zone before the spring growing season.

## Snow mold diseases

Two snow mold diseases affect turf in northern zones (4–6) during extended snow cover:

**Gray snow mold (Typhula blight):** Grayish-white mycelial mats visible when snow melts. Favors heavy snow cover over green turf for 3+ weeks. Affected areas appear tan or bleached in circular or irregular patches. Most cool-season grasses recover without treatment if snow mold damage is caught early.

**Pink snow mold (Microdochium patch):** Salmon-pink mycelial growth; does not require snow cover. Active from fall through early spring in cool, wet conditions.

**Prevention:**
- Do not fertilize with nitrogen in late fall (no applications after ground is nearly frozen)
- Mow turf to normal height before snowfall — extremely long grass has higher snow mold susceptibility
- Avoid packing snow into large piles on turf — the extended snow cover under piles creates ideal conditions

**Treatment after snow melts:**
- Rake affected areas lightly to improve air circulation
- Most gray snow mold will recover as temperatures rise and the area dries
- Fungicide is not typically necessary for homeowner lawns except in areas with recurring severe infection

## Pre-spring checklist

As winter concludes (late February – March by zone), before spring growth begins:

1. **Walk the lawn:** Document bare spots, disease patches, and traffic damage
2. **Check mower and equipment:** Service mower before first use of season
3. **Review fall fertilizer records:** Determine if fall program was complete or incomplete
4. **Order seed:** If overseeding bare spots in fall, have seed ready
5. **Check crabgrass pre-emergent timing:** Know your zone's soil temperature threshold — have product on hand before needed
