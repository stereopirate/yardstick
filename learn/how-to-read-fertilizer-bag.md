A bag of fertilizer gives you all the information you need — if you know how to read it. The three bold numbers on the front are the guaranteed analysis: nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P₂O₅), and potassium (K₂O), listed in that order. Everything else on the label builds from those three numbers.

## The three numbers: N-P-K

A bag labeled **24-0-12** contains:
- **24%** nitrogen by weight
- **0%** phosphate (P₂O₅) by weight
- **12%** potassium (K₂O) by weight

The percentages are by weight of the total bag contents. A 50-lb bag of 24-0-12 contains 12 lbs of actual nitrogen (50 × 0.24 = 12), zero phosphate, and 6 lbs of potassium oxide.

The remaining percentage (64% in this case) is filler, carrier material, and other minor nutrients — not active plant nutrients.

## Nitrogen: the most important number

Nitrogen (N) drives leaf growth, deep green color, and turf density. It is the nutrient lawns consume in the largest quantity and the number most lawn care decisions are built around.

**Why N matters most:**
Lawn fertilizer application rates are calculated in pounds of actual nitrogen per 1,000 square feet. A standard lawn fertilizer application delivers 0.5–1.0 lb of nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft per application. Purdue Extension recommends this as the basis for all turf fertilization planning.

Too much nitrogen applied too fast causes rapid, lush growth that is susceptible to disease, requires more frequent mowing, and can burn turf in hot weather. Too little nitrogen leads to thin, pale turf that is vulnerable to weed invasion and slow to recover from stress.

## Phosphorus and when you need it

Phosphorus (P, listed as P₂O₅) primarily supports root development and seedling establishment. For established lawns with adequate soil phosphorus levels, it is often not needed.

**When to use phosphorus:**
- New lawn establishment (seed or sod)
- Overseeding thin or bare areas
- When a soil test shows phosphorus deficiency

**When to skip phosphorus:**
Most established lawns in the US do not need supplemental phosphorus. Many states (including Minnesota and several Mid-Atlantic states) now restrict phosphorus in fertilizers applied to established lawns due to runoff concerns. Check your local regulations.

A soil test is the definitive way to know if your lawn needs phosphorus. Without a soil test, selecting a fertilizer with low or zero phosphorus (like 24-0-12 or 32-0-8) is reasonable for lawn maintenance applications.

## Potassium for stress tolerance

Potassium (K, listed as K₂O) does not drive visible growth like nitrogen but plays a critical role in:
- Drought tolerance
- Cold hardiness and winter survival
- Disease resistance
- Cell wall strength

Penn State Extension recommends potassium at roughly half the nitrogen rate for most lawn maintenance applications. A fertilizer with an N:K ratio of 2:1 or 3:1 is a reasonable starting point for most lawn types. Fall applications with higher potassium help cool-season grasses harden off for winter.

## Slow-release vs. fast-release nitrogen

Fertilizers with the same N-P-K numbers can behave very differently depending on whether the nitrogen is fast-release (water-soluble) or slow-release (coated or organic).

**Fast-release (soluble) nitrogen:**
Absorbed quickly. Produces rapid green response within 3–5 days. Higher risk of burn if over-applied or applied in heat. Less expensive. Examples: ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate, urea.

**Slow-release nitrogen:**
Coated granules (sulfur-coated urea, polymer-coated) release nitrogen over 6–12 weeks. Lower burn risk. Provides more even, sustained growth. More expensive. University of Minnesota Extension recommends slow-release nitrogen for summer applications to reduce heat stress risk.

Look for "WIN" (water-insoluble nitrogen) percentage on the label — this represents the slow-release fraction. A fertilizer with 30–50% WIN provides a good balance of quick green-up and sustained feeding.

## Reading the rest of the label

Beyond N-P-K, the fertilizer label contains important information:

- **Application rate:** Usually given as lbs per 1,000 sq ft or square footage coverage per bag
- **Application timing:** "For use on established lawns" or "for seeding" — establishes intended use
- **Watering instructions:** Most granular fertilizers should be watered in within 24 hours of application
- **State restrictions:** Some states restrict phosphorus applications to established lawns
- **Weed-and-feed products:** Contain herbicide combined with fertilizer — application timing is driven by the herbicide requirements, not just fertilizer needs
