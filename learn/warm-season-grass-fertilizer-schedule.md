Warm-season grasses grow actively in summer and go dormant when temperatures fall below 50–55°F in fall. Their fertilizer calendar is the opposite of cool-season grasses: fertilize during active summer growth, stop in late summer/fall, and never fertilize dormant or semi-dormant turf.

## Warm-season growth cycle

The key timing rule for warm-season fertilization:

- **Do fertilize:** When the lawn is fully green and actively growing (late spring through summer)
- **Do not fertilize:** While the lawn is dormant or in early spring green-up transition
- **Stop fertilizing:** 6–8 weeks before the average first frost date for your zone

Nitrogen applied to dormant or semi-dormant warm-season grass is largely wasted. The turf cannot take it up efficiently, and excess nitrogen left in the soil can stimulate early spring green-up attempts that are then damaged by late frost.

The "50% green" rule is the most reliable trigger. Soil temperature at 2 inches should be consistently above 65°F before the first fertilizer application. UGA Extension notes that fertilizing before this threshold stimulates weed germination more than grass growth.

## Fertilizer rates by grass type

**Bermudagrass:** The highest-nitrogen warm-season grass. Active growing-season bermuda benefits from 3–5 lbs of actual N per 1,000 sq ft annually, split across 4–6 applications. Golf-course quality bermuda receives even more, but for residential lawns, 3–4 lbs is appropriate. Use a 3-1-2 ratio fertilizer (N-P-K) or similar.

**Zoysiagrass:** More moderate nitrogen needs than bermuda. 2–4 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft annually is typical. Zoysia grows more slowly and does not recover from scalping or stress as quickly as bermuda — avoid high nitrogen rates that force growth the grass cannot sustain.

**St. Augustinegrass:** 2–4 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft annually in 2–3 split applications. Texas A&M AgriLife recommends 1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft every 8 weeks during the growing season. Use a fertilizer with iron (Fe) to maintain color without excess nitrogen. St. Augustine is particularly susceptible to chinch bug damage when over-fertilized with nitrogen.

**Centipedegrass:** The exception — centipede needs the least nitrogen of any common lawn grass. 1–2 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft annually is the Clemson Extension recommendation. Excessive nitrogen causes iron chlorosis (yellowing), scalping sensitivity, and "centipede decline" — a deterioration pattern that takes years to recover from.

## Centipedegrass warning: less is more

Centipedegrass is a low-maintenance grass that performs best when treated that way. The most common centipede management mistake is over-fertilizing.

Clemson Extension identifies "centipede decline" as the most common problem they see in centipede lawns — and the most common cause is excess nitrogen. Symptoms include yellowing, thinning patches, and failure to recover from drought or winter. Recovery takes 2–3 years of minimal inputs.

For centipede: apply 1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft once in late spring (after the lawn is fully green), and optionally a second 0.5 lb N application in early summer. Do not apply after July in zones 7–8. Do not apply potassium at high rates either — centipede is also sensitive to potassium excess.

## Fall feeding dos and don'ts

**Do apply potassium in early fall.** A fall potassium application (without nitrogen) improves cold hardiness and winter survival. Use a product like 0-0-60 (muriate of potash) or a maintenance fertilizer with high K and low N at 0.5–1.0 lb K₂O per 1,000 sq ft in early September (zones 7–8) or October (zones 8–9).

**Do not apply nitrogen after the last application window.** Fall nitrogen forces green tissue that freezes in early cold snaps and extends the weed germination window.

**Do apply iron for late-season color.** If your warm-season lawn starts to yellow in early fall, iron sulfate or chelated iron gives a green response without nitrogen-driven growth. This is the appropriate tool for fall color in warm-season turf.
